Learning Objectives

What You Will Learn

In this lesson, you will learn how to express your opinions confidently in English. By the end of this session, you will be able to use the correct vocabulary, apply proper pronunciation, and employ a range of opinion expressions in real conversations.

  • Identify and use key vocabulary related to opinion and discussion
  • Apply expressions for giving, agreeing with, and disagreeing with opinions
  • Speak with clarity and appropriate confidence in group discussions
  • Respond respectfully to other people's perspectives
  • Practice real speaking scenarios and reflective thinking

Speaking Focus

This lesson centers on spoken English — practicing how to articulate ideas aloud with confidence and correct structure.

Real-World Context

Discussions on school life, social issues, and community topics — relevant situations Grade 12 students face every day.

Vocabulary

Key Words to Know

Click a card to expand it. Tap the speaker icon to hear pronunciation.

01

Opinion

noun

A personal view or judgment about something, not necessarily based on fact.

"In my opinion, students should wear uniforms."

02

Agree

verb

To have the same opinion or to accept something as correct or acceptable.

"I agree with your point about homework."

03

Disagree

verb

To have a different opinion; to think that something is wrong or unacceptable.

"I disagree with the idea of banning mobile phones."

04

Perspective

noun

A particular attitude toward or way of regarding something; a point of view.

"From my perspective, online learning has many benefits."

05

Argument

noun

A reason or set of reasons given to support a view or opinion in a discussion.

"Her argument about climate change was very convincing."

06

Confidence

noun

The feeling of being certain about your ability to do something or say something.

"She spoke with great confidence during the debate."

Expressions

Opinion Expressions

Click each expression card to see how it's used in context.

"In my opinion…"
Used to introduce a personal view.
"In my opinion, social media has more negative effects on students."
"I think that…"
A common and natural way to share your thoughts.
"I think that students should learn financial literacy."
"I believe…"
Expresses a strong personal conviction.
"I believe every student deserves equal access to education."
"From my point of view…"
Signals a subjective perspective.
"From my point of view, homework should be limited to one hour."
"I agree because…"
Shows agreement and provides a reason.
"I agree because exercise really does improve concentration in class."
"That's a good point."
Acknowledges and validates someone's argument.
"That's a good point. I hadn't thought of it that way."
"I completely agree with…"
Shows strong agreement with a person or idea.
"I completely agree with your statement about school uniforms."
"You're absolutely right."
Emphatic agreement that validates the speaker.
"You're absolutely right — teamwork is essential in the workplace."
"I disagree with…"
Politely states that you hold a different view.
"I disagree with the idea that online classes are more effective."
"From my perspective…"
Offers an alternative viewpoint respectfully.
"From my perspective, the policy actually causes more problems."
"I see it differently."
A soft way to signal disagreement without confrontation.
"I see it differently — I think we need stricter rules."
"I'm not sure I agree…"
A tentative, polite form of disagreement.
"I'm not sure I agree with banning all gadgets in school."
